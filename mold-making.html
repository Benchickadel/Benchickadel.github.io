I started by connecting curves to a 7 sided polygon.  I followed a pretty basic tutorial but helpful in creating the 'brilliant cut' diamond.  I just wanted something with odd sides. Using points and switching views a lot helped speed this along.

Using the "make surface from 3 or 4 curves, I created 1 set of faces on the pavilion of the diamond (lower half of the diamond.  I then used a polar array to create all the faces.  Didn't work on this one.  I did a ton of eyeballing and it was super gappy. Back to the drawing board.  I probably made two before it worked correctly.

I created the crown and was already planning my mold would be split at the girdle or center of the diamond.  I used a 56 sided polygon for the bas of each.  The problem was that when I test printed a positive it was too rounded.  I wanted a more faceted look to reflect light off of the surface.

I reduced the girdle to a 14-sided polygon and test printed.

Above is a standard setting on the print. It was ok, but probably lots of work to smooth out.  I went ahead and designed the mold with this model hoping that a finer quality print would work out.

For the mold I started with a box and a tapered interior box. I wanted the mold to release as easily as possible from the PLA print.

I enlarged on half of the keys in the corners.  For the 3D printed mold it is the ones that are convex. This way they will create a slightly larger concave hole for the other part of the mold to fit into.

The model would not boolean union together at all. Then I turned on the ghosted rendering and noticed the floor of the mold was reversed. Flipped the direction and it union-ed together fine.  I think this happened because I extruded a tapered surface. If I had flipped the original surface I think this wouldn't have happened.

Both models were closed polysurfaces and exported as stl files fine.

crown and plug print, 0.12mm layer height, PLA

I printed these separate to be able to remove the mold.  There is a slight indentation on the table of the crown to fit the printed plug into mold and have it stay there wil it is being cast.

This is the mold for the plug.  The rectangle is designed to fit the rectangle formed by the PLA plug above.

I started with trying to do a little measurement of water displacement to estimate how much to mix. This was close but I really should have mixed a bit more.

Measured out part A and B and stirred and stirred and stirred. and stirred

I almost filled the pavilion and the crown parts of the mold and then ran out of smooth-sil.  I quickly mixed another batch and was able to fill all the molds.  I tapped the board the molds rested on to release all the bubbles.

I was surprised how easily the molds released from the PLA.  I was worried and added a string loop on one of the molds while it was curing.  It actually helped but in the end wasn't necessary.

First casting.  I used tempered dark chocolate and am really messy with melted dark chocolate

I had extra chocolate and poured it in a turkey mold. Because who doesn't want a chocolate turkey in may. I used the turkey to check when the chocolate was set. Surprise! I added marshmallow inside but it floated to the top .  The casting and shell thickness was pretty good though.

Second casting went better.  This was hollow.  I filled the pavilion and then places the top and plug on and shook the mold.

In the future, for this mold, I think I'd like to smooth the PLA print.  Its a pretty fine print but I think the lines are distracting.
